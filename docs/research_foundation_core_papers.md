## One-Sentence Foundation

Caregiving matters for child development, but its effects are not uniform; the dissertation asks whether caregiving becomes developmentally effective when the child is positioned to take up, initiate, and reorganize support through joint attention and dyadic behavioral-neural coordination.

## Best Story

The field has strong but separated literatures on responsive caregiving, scaffolding, dynamic systems, joint attention, parent-child synchrony, and fNIRS hyperscanning. Together, these literatures support a more precise question than "Does caregiving matter?" They point toward a calibration problem: when, for whom, and through what dyadic organization does caregiving become functionally linked to children's performance and developmental change? The dissertation's contribution is to treat child social attention and dyadic neural organization not as side variables, but as the developmental conditions under which caregiving support is translated into child outcomes.

## Argument Spine

### 1. Caregiving is a real developmental context, not a decorative background variable.

The core caregiving papers support a broad consensus: parental sensitivity, responsiveness, scaffolding, tutoring, synchrony, and interaction quality are linked to children's cognitive, language, behavioral, and socioemotional outcomes.

Use this as the opening consensus. The dissertation does not need to prove that caregiving matters. It needs to explain why caregiving matters unevenly.

### 2. Dynamic and transactional theories make heterogeneity expected.

Dynamic systems and transactional-development papers argue that development emerges through reciprocal, time-dependent, context-sensitive interaction. This makes average caregiving effects theoretically incomplete. If development is dynamic, the important empirical question is not only whether caregiving predicts outcomes, but where in developmental space the caregiving pathway becomes active.

### 3. Child agency needs an observable handle.

"Child agency" is too abstract to carry the empirical argument by itself. The stronger operational move is to use joint attention as the observable form of child participation in social learning. RJA captures the child's capacity to take up another person's attentional bid; IJA captures the child's active initiation of shared attention.

### 4. RJA and IJA are not interchangeable.

The RJA/IJA papers justify treating responding and initiating as distinct developmental pathways. RJA is especially useful for asking when the child can receive, follow, and use caregiver support. IJA is useful for asking when the child actively reorganizes the interactional field.

### 5. Heterogeneity is the phenomenon, not a nuisance.

The foundation should frame developmental variability as theoretically meaningful. Caregiving effects may appear weak, inconsistent, or modest at the average level because the pathway only opens under particular child, context, or dyadic states.

### 6. Neural synchrony extends the model, but synchrony alone is not enough.

Parent-child hyperscanning studies show that dyadic neural organization can be measured during naturalistic and structured interaction. However, synchrony mostly indexes coupling or similarity. It does not by itself say who is influencing whom, whether the child is taking up support, or whether the dyad is organized around parent-led or child-led regulation.

### 7. Directionality is the methodological frontier.

MVGC and Granger-causality papers provide a defensible way to ask directional questions about influence. The key claim should be careful: the field has the tools and the theoretical need for parent-child neural directionality, but direct developmental evidence is still limited. That limitation is a strength of the dissertation rationale.

### 8. Longitudinal recalibration is the integrative contribution.

The dissertation-level move is to connect short-timescale dyadic organization with longer-timescale developmental change. The strongest version of the project asks whether caregiving effectiveness is recalibrated by child joint attention and parent-child directional organization across development.

## Core Construct Map

|---|---|---|
| Responsive caregiving | Predictor / developmental context | Establishes support, sensitivity, scaffolding, and regulatory input. |
| Scaffolding | Mechanism of support | Caregiver help is useful when it fits what the child cannot yet do independently. |

## Central Research Question

When is responsive caregiving translated into child performance and developmental change, and is that translation calibrated by children's joint-attention pathway and parent-child directional organization?

## Stronger Version

Does child joint attention mark a developmental readiness state that changes the functional link between caregiving support and child outcomes, and do parent-to-child versus child-to-parent neural dynamics explain how that link is organized during interaction?

## Competing Hypotheses

| Hypothesis | Prediction | What would weaken it |
|---|---|---|
| H1: Caregiving-main-effect model | Responsive caregiving predicts better performance across children and contexts, regardless of RJA/IJA or neural directionality. | Effects appear only in specific joint-attention or dyadic-organization states. |
| H2: Child-uptake calibration model | Caregiving predicts performance most strongly when children show higher RJA or better capacity to take up caregiver bids. | RJA does not moderate caregiving effects, or IJA/other factors fully explain the pattern. |
| H3: Child-led reorganization model | IJA or child-to-parent directionality marks moments when children actively shape the interaction, altering how caregiver support functions. | Child-led measures do not predict caregiving effectiveness or are reducible to age/ability. |
| H4: Context-specific organization model | Structured tasks and free play reveal different calibration patterns. Parent-led support may matter more in structured challenge; child-led organization may matter more in free play. | The same pattern appears across contexts with no meaningful task differences. |
| H5: Artifact or general maturity model | Apparent calibration reflects age, baseline ability, language, SES, motion/data quality, or task difficulty rather than dyadic process. | Effects remain after accounting for these confounds and appear in theoretically specific pathways. |

## Good Question Card

**Working title:** Developmental calibration of caregiving through joint attention and dyadic neural directionality

**Research question:** Under what child and dyadic conditions does responsive caregiving become functionally linked to children's task performance and developmental change?

**Why it matters:** It moves the project beyond the broad claim that caregiving matters toward a more precise model of when caregiving support is taken up, reorganized, and translated into development.

**Core assumption challenged:** The dominant average-effect model assumes caregiving influence can be estimated as a stable parent-to-child effect. The dissertation challenges this by treating child participation and dyadic organization as conditions of influence.

**Competing hypotheses:** Caregiving main effect; child-uptake calibration; child-led reorganization; context-specific organization; artifact/general maturity.

**Discriminating observation or experiment:** Compare whether caregiving-outcome links change across RJA, IJA, structured/free-play context, and parent-to-child versus child-to-parent neural directionality.

**What would falsify it:** If caregiving predicts outcomes equally across all child attention states, dyadic contexts, and directionality patterns, the calibration model is unnecessary.

**Two-week pilot:** Use existing coded or pilot data to test one narrow model: caregiving predicting task performance with RJA/IJA as moderators, controlling for age and baseline ability. If neural data are ready, add a parallel exploratory model for parent-to-child and child-to-parent directionality.

**Data/resources needed:** Observed caregiving, RJA/IJA coding, child task outcome, age/developmental covariates, structured/free-play labels, and fNIRS/MVGC outputs if available.

**Strongest reviewer objection:** The model may be too integrative and risks treating correlated dyadic markers as mechanism. The repair is to predefine a small number of discriminating predictions and keep causal language calibrated.

**Best next action:** Build a foundation table linking each study hypothesis to the exact construct, measure, paper cluster, and falsifier.

## Dissertation Claim Ladder

1. Caregiving is a well-supported developmental context.
2. Caregiving effects are heterogeneous, and this heterogeneity is theoretically expected.
3. Dynamic and transactional theories require models of reciprocal, time-sensitive organization.
4. Joint attention provides an observable child-participation pathway.
5. RJA and IJA should be modeled separately because they represent different positions in the interaction.
6. Parent-child neural synchrony shows dyadic organization but cannot fully answer influence or leadership questions.
7. Directionality methods allow the dissertation to test a more transactional version of the model.
8. The dissertation's integrative contribution is a calibrated account of when caregiving support is translated into child performance and developmental change.

## Recommended Framing Language

Use:

> Prior work shows that caregiving, joint attention, and parent-child neural organization each matter for development, but these literatures remain partially separated. The present study asks whether children's joint attention and dyadic neural directionality help explain when caregiving support becomes functionally linked to child performance.

Avoid:

> No one has studied this.

Better:

> Prior studies provide the component evidence, but fewer studies integrate observed caregiving, RJA/IJA, task performance, interaction context, and directional neural organization in the same developmental design.

Avoid:

> Neural directionality proves parent-to-child causal influence.

Better:

> Directionality analyses provide a temporally ordered estimate of influence that is better aligned with transactional theory than synchrony alone, while still requiring careful control and interpretation.

## Review Risks and Repairs

| Risk | Why it matters | Repair |
|---|---|---|
| Too many constructs | The project may look overbuilt. | Use calibration as the single organizing contribution. |
| Causal overclaiming | Observational dyadic data are vulnerable to confounds. | Use "linked with," "organized by," "calibrated by," and reserve causal claims for designs that support them. |
| RJA/IJA operational inconsistency | Joint attention is measured differently across studies. | Define coding rules clearly and justify RJA/IJA as separate pathways. |
| Synchrony/directionality ambiguity | Neural coupling can reflect task, movement, shared stimulus, or artifact. | Include controls, quality checks, context comparisons, and cautious interpretation. |
| Heterogeneity looks post hoc | Sliding/local models can seem exploratory. | Predefine developmental windows or decision rules; use robustness checks. |
